Dynasties of wealth: The preservation of wealth within families

How do families manage to maintain their position at the top of the wealth distribution over more than a century?

So what?

  • Mobility within the wealt elite? “each class resembles a hotel […] always full, but always of different people” (Schumpeter 1972)
  • Normative: Performance and not birth should influence wealth position
  • Social cohesion at risk (Toft and Hansen 2022)
  • Social background of wealth elite matters for politics we get (Reeves and Friedman 2024)

  • Larger project describing the super-rich in Germany - Article 1: Today’s super-rich - Historical projects, wealth accumulated partly over several generations - New project: how families manage to maintain their position at the top of the wealth distribution over more than 100 years. I am interested in the influence of social structure and in particular how family relationships and membership of the aristocracy contribute to the preservation of wealth. - Why is it relevant to look at whether and how the wealth elite maintains its position over a century? - 1. to understand the role of the family as an economic unit. Does the family play a relevant role in the (re)production of wealth and the wealth elite? - 2. normative justification. Meritocracy: merit, not birth, determines position in wealth distribution. - 3.economic resources distributed inefficiently. no personal change within the elite–> social closure–> no longer understands social problems and thus jeopardises social cohesion.

Previous research

  • Family as a relevant economic unit (Farrell 1993a)

  • Intergenerational wealth mobility low (Hansen 2014; Clark and Cummins 2015; Pfeffer and Killewald 2018; Hällsten and Kolk 2023; Toft and Hansen 2022)

    • Despite institutional changes: Wealth mobility constant in GB (1858-2012) (Clark and Cummins 2015)

    • Persistence in wealth, income and elite occupations in Italy (Barone and Mocetti 2020)

    • Germany: About 8% of today’s 1000 largest fortunes can be traced back to fortunes from the 1913 wealth lists (Tisch and Ischinsky 2023)

    • Zurich: About 20% of 1952’ top 0.1% have ancestors within top 0.1% in 1890, yearly survival rate of 97.5% (Legentilhomme and Leimgruber 2023)

→ Research gap: Mechanisms of wealth preservation of families, e.g. family ties within the elite

What can we learn from previous research on wealth preservation? - Franziska Wiest: managing family feelings plays a critical role in wealth reproduction - Wealth mobility is low, especially at the upper and lower ends of the wealth distribution. - Register data (Norway) and survey data (up to three generations). - Rare surnames to estimate wealth mobility over several generations or even centuries. - Clark & Cummins look at wealth mobility in the UK and show that it has remained constant over the last 5 generations despite institutional changes. [When he groups surnames by wealth level, there is less mobility than when he looks at individual surnames] - Barone & Mocetti look at the city of Florence and show an enormous persistence in income, wealth and elite occupations between 1427 and 2011. But what are the socio-structural mechanisms of wealth preservation? My contribution: 1. to what extent is there social mobility within the wealth elite in Germany? 2. explain the persistence of wealth through socio-structural factors. 3. family ties within the wealth and political elite 4. by looking at the connection between the wealth elite and the political elite, I also contribute to one of the most important topics of elite literature, namely whether there was a homogeneous elite. 5. shifting the perspective to kinship structures as social groups, where social reproduction works at the group level, appears to be a fruitful endeavor, and it should be a future aim to identify more kin group-level characteristics that explain persistence. (Hällsten und Kolk 2020)

  • Family as a central institution for the reproduction of social inequality (Beckert 2022; Wiest 2025; Mare 2011; O’Brien 2023; Pfeffer and Killewald 2018)
  • Reproduction of the wealth elite
    • Direct intergenerational transfers

    • Social closure through marriage (Farrell 1993b)
    • Economic alliance building (Zeitlin 1974)

Hypotheses

Hypothesis 1: Members of the economic elite are more likely to have descendants on today’s rich list than non-members

→ Socialisation in business families and economic alliances (Augustine 2003)

Hypothesis 2: The more interconnected a rich individual is with other rich individuals through familial ties, the greater the likelihood their descendants will appear on today’s rich lists

→ Strategic family alliances to consolidate the social position (Augustine 1994; Farrell 1993b; Zeitlin 1974)

Hypothesis 3: Members of the political elite are more likely to have descendants on today’s rich list than non-members

→ Stealth politics (Page, Seawright, and Lacombe 2018)

  • Hypotheses - argument: strategic alliances are necessary to maintain the position at the top of the wealth distribution. - Good family network. Just as a good network is important for an individual to accumulate wealth over the course of their life, I expect that it is also important for families to have a good family network. - “imprinting effect” of belonging to the core of the elite. - Qualitative studies such as Betty Farrell’s for Boston in the 19th century point to the importance of the family network. And I would like to look at whether a good family network at the beginning of the 20th century has long-term consequences. - What is the imprinting effect? Name? - The imprinting effect is a positive correlation between the level of wealth and the chances of wealth preservation. Charles Wright Mills argues in his book “The power elite” that wealth brings with it an accumulation advantage. The more strategic the economic position and the richer a person is, the easier it is to accumulate even more wealth: “The more he has, the greater his credit”
